## The tool graveyard

You signed up for the project management tool because it looked clean and everyone on Twitter was recommending it. Then you added the CRM because your sales pipeline was getting hard to track. Then the form builder, because the CRM did not handle intake well. Then the automation tool to connect all three.

You are now paying for four subscriptions. Your team uses two of them. The data lives in all of them. Nothing talks to anything else without breaking.

This is not a sign that you bought bad software. It is a sign that you bought software built for someone else's business and tried to make it fit yours. That is a different problem, and it has a different solution.

## When SaaS is exactly the right answer

I want to start here, because most articles like this one skip it.

SaaS works extremely well when your problem is standard. Accounting, payroll, email marketing, scheduling, video calls: these are solved problems. The workflows are the same across industries, the edge cases are already handled, and the products have had years of refinement. Buying a custom accounting system in 2026 would be a serious mistake.

SaaS also makes sense when you are early. If you are still figuring out how your business actually operates, locking into a custom build is premature. You will build the wrong thing. Start with off-the-shelf tools, learn how you actually work, then build something specific once you know what specific means.

And SaaS makes sense when your volume is low. If you process twenty client requests a month and your current setup handles it without real pain, the economics of a custom build do not add up yet. The tool does not need to be perfect. It needs to be good enough for where you are.

If any of that describes you right now, stop reading and go find the right SaaS product for your problem. There is no shame in it.

## When SaaS starts failing you

Here is where it gets interesting. There are four signals I look for when someone tells me their tools are not working.

**You are running your process around the tool instead of the other way around.** This is the most common one. You added a step to your workflow not because it made sense, but because the software required it. You renamed things to match the software's terminology. Your team learned a workaround on day one and has been using it ever since. When the tool is shaping how you work rather than supporting how you work, you are in the wrong tool.

**You are paying for a large product to use a small part of it.** Some platforms sell you fifty features when you need four. The price reflects the full product. Your usage does not. And the four features you actually need are buried under interface designed for companies that use all fifty. You end up paying for complexity that slows you down.

**Your data is split across multiple platforms with no clean source of truth.** Client records in the CRM. Job status in the project tool. Invoices in the accounting platform. Notes in a shared doc. When someone asks a simple question, like where is this client in the process, the answer requires checking three places. That is not a workflow problem. That is a data architecture problem. Off-the-shelf tools are not designed to solve it.

**Integration is becoming a part-time job.** You are spending real time maintaining the connections between tools. The Zapier flows break when a platform updates. Someone has to fix it. That person is either you or someone on your team who has better things to do. When integration maintenance becomes a recurring task, the cost of fragmentation has already exceeded most people's estimates.

## What custom actually costs

This is where most conversations stall, because the numbers people have in their head are usually wrong in both directions.

Some founders assume custom software means a six-figure project and eighteen months of development. That is enterprise software. That is not what most growing businesses need.

A focused custom tool, built to solve one or two specific problems, typically runs between three thousand and fifteen thousand euros depending on complexity. A client intake system with a backend dashboard: on the lower end. A full operational platform with user roles, reporting, and integrations: higher. The range is wide, but it is not unlimited.

The more useful comparison is not custom vs SaaS on price alone. It is custom vs the total cost of your current setup: subscriptions, integration tools, the hours your team spends working around limitations, the leads that fall through because the handoff between tools failed, and the developer time you spend maintaining glue code between platforms.

When you add that up honestly, the gap closes faster than most people expect.

The other thing worth knowing: custom does not mean starting from scratch every time. Good development builds on existing infrastructure. A project that might sound complex is often simpler than it appears once someone experienced looks at the actual requirements.

## Three questions to ask before you decide

I use these with every founder who comes to me unsure whether to build or buy.

**Is your process genuinely different from industry standard?** If the way you acquire clients, deliver work, and manage relationships follows a recognizable pattern, there is probably a SaaS product designed for it. If your process has real distinctions, either because of your industry, your model, or your clients, that is where custom starts making sense.

**What is the real cost of the status quo?** Not the subscription cost. The total cost. Time lost to coordination, errors that create rework, clients who went quiet because nobody followed up in time, team members who are frustrated by tools that do not fit. If that number is vague, spend an hour making it concrete before making any decision.

**Are you solving a permanent problem or a temporary one?** If your current pain is a phase you are passing through, a SaaS workaround might be good enough until you are through it. If the problem is structural, meaning it will only grow as the business grows, that changes the calculus. Structural problems do not get better with temporary solutions. They get more expensive.

## What I actually do in these conversations

When someone comes to me wondering whether to build something, the first thing I do is try to talk them out of it.

Not because I do not want the work. Because the wrong build is worse than no build. If a SaaS product solves your problem cleanly and you can be up and running in a week, that is the right answer. I will tell you which one to look at.

If we get through that conversation and it becomes clear that your problem is specific enough, structural enough, and costly enough that a custom tool makes sense, then I walk you through what that tool would actually look like and what it would cost to build it.

That is the only honest way to have this conversation.
